Named in honour of Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela — whose clan name Madiba became synonymous with the conviction that dialogue, not dogma, shapes the arc of history — this Club exists to convene the kinds of conversations that refuse easy answers.

For its inaugural Manchester edition, The Madiba Club assembles five panellists whose careers span the continuum from grassroots youth empowerment to sovereign credit ratings, from the corridors of Abuja's diplomatic service to the lecture halls of one of the world's great universities. Together, they will interrogate the economic architecture, political imagination, and cultural self-belief required for Africa to graduate from participant in the global order to architect of it.

The Panel

Distinguished Speakers

Speaker I

Stanislas Zézé-Bayard

CEO & Chairman, Bloomfield Investment Corporation · University of Michigan MPA · Chevalier de l'Ordre National de Côte d'Ivoire
When Stanislas Zézé founded Bloomfield Investment Corporation in 2007, Africa's Francophone capital markets had no indigenous credit-rating agency. International firms — Standard & Poor's, Moody's, Fitch — held an unchallenged monopoly over the narratives that determined which African sovereigns and corporates were deemed creditworthy. From Abidjan, Zézé built the first Pan-African rating agency operating in West and Central Africa, pioneering a methodology anchored in local-currency baskets rather than dollar-denominated benchmarks alone. That single methodological shift rewrote how risk is perceived across the UEMOA zone.
His trajectory — from credit risk director at the National Bank of Detroit, through the World Bank's operational risk unit in Washington, to senior country risk officer at the African Development Bank — gave him a panoramic view of how capital flows are gatekept. At Shell Oil Africa, he directed regional credit across West and Central Africa before returning to entrepreneurship. Today, Bloomfield's annual Country Risk Conferences have become decisive calendar events for Francophone investors and policymakers.
Known across Abidjan for his trademark red socks — a flamboyant signature in the otherwise sober world of sovereign ratings — Zézé recounts in his autobiography, The Man with the Red Socks, that when he first proposed the Bloomfield project, the only person who believed in him was his wife, Jeanne. Forbes Afrique put him on its cover in April 2016. In 2018, Financial Afrik crowned him Financier of the Year. Proof, perhaps, that the most disruptive ideas begin with a single believer.

Speaker II

Dr Alessia Isopi

Lecturer in Development Economics, University of Manchester · PhD, Università di Roma 'Tor Vergata' · Marie Curie Research Fellow
Dr Isopi belongs to a rare category of development economist: one who can traverse the arid terrain of econometric modelling and emerge on the other side with narratives that are lucid, urgent, and startlingly human. Her research at Manchester spans development economics, information asymmetries, and experimental economics, with a particular strength in translating abstract theory into concrete policy insight. Her scholarship carries the rigour of empirical evidence and the clarity of someone who has spent over a decade making complex ideas accessible to diverse audiences.
Trained at LUISS 'Guido Carli' in Rome before earning her doctorate at Tor Vergata, she held a prestigious Marie Curie Intra-European Fellowship at the University of Nottingham. Her published work ranges from aid allocation and corruption to the political economy of institutions. She sits within a department at Manchester that traces its development economics lineage to W. Arthur Lewis and Hans Singer — pioneers of the discipline itself.
Her most celebrated paper may also be her most deliciously unexpected: published in the Journal of Economic History, "Origins of the Sicilian Mafia: The Market for Lemons" argues that the mafia emerged not from political intrigue, but from the booming 19th-century citrus trade — lemon growers needed protection, and the Cosa Nostra obliged. It is a masterclass in how economic structures produce institutional pathologies — a lens she will bring to Africa's own resource-governance dilemmas.

Speaker III

H.E. Ambassador Bello A. Jaye

Head of Industry, Trade and Investment, Nigerian High Commission, London · / Mr Magaji Barda
Ambassador Jaye occupies the intersection where statecraft meets market access. As Head of Industry, Trade and Investment at the Nigerian High Commission in London, he serves as the principal diplomatic interface between Nigeria's economic ambitions and British capital, trade corridors, and institutional partnerships. His portfolio encompasses everything from championing the UK's Developing Countries Trading Scheme — which grants duty-free access to nearly three thousand Nigerian goods — to brokering investment pathways in agriculture, energy, technology, and healthcare.
In a role that demands equal fluency in boardrooms and policy chambers, Ambassador Jaye has distinguished himself as an advocate for cross-border trade that is not merely transactional but structurally transformative. His recent engagements have emphasised the importance of diaspora investment as a catalyst for domestic industrial development, and he has been vocal about the reforms needed to attract patient, productive foreign direct investment rather than speculative inflows.
In April 2025, Ambassador Jaye made headlines for hosting a business meeting at the Nigerian High Commission where the keynote speaker was a sixteen-year-old youth ambassador, who delivered a speech on why young Black British people should reconnect with their roots and explore business opportunities across both the UK and Nigeria. It was a gesture that said everything about his philosophy: that the future of trade diplomacy belongs not only to those who have arrived, but to those who are arriving.

Speaker IV

Prof. Carl Death

Senior Lecturer in International Political Economy, University of Manchester · Author, African Climate Futures (OUP, 2025)
Carl Death has spent two decades at the precise collision point between African politics, environmental governance, and global power structures. His research — conducted across South Africa, Tanzania, and at institutions including WISER (Wits), Stellenbosch, and KwaZulu-Natal — draws on Foucauldian governmentality theory to reveal how environmental policies and development discourses are weaponised, negotiated, and resisted across the continent.
At Manchester, he convenes the BISA Africa and International Studies working group and serves as Associate Editor of International Relations. His latest book, African Climate Futures (Oxford University Press, 2025), examines how climate-changed futures are being imagined across the continent — blending political analysis with African speculative fiction and futurist narratives.
What distinguishes Death from conventional IR scholars is an intellectual fearlessness that spans registers: he is as likely to analyse a UN summit through the lens of biopolitics as he is to publish a peer-reviewed article on Trevor Noah's stand-up comedy as a form of political resistance. His paper on "ambivalent mockery" in Noah's performances argued that comedy can simultaneously inhabit and subvert dominant power relations — a framework that proves unexpectedly productive when applied to Africa's own diplomatic posture on the world stage.

Speaker V

Edenamiuki Aiguobasinmwin

Founder, Elevate Young Minds & Young Creative Leaders · Member, Commonwealth Youth for Development and Peace · Arts Council England North Area Board Member
Known to his community simply as Namiuki, Edenamiuki Aiguobasinmwin is a creative practitioner, social entrepreneur, and institution-builder who uses the arts as a vehicle for meaningful social transformation. Since founding Elevate Young Minds in Manchester — a Black-led, solution-driven youth organisation — he has designed pathways for young people across the arts, enterprise, and leadership, operating from Monsall, one of Greater Manchester's most deprived communities.
His reach, however, is anything but local. Through the Young Creative Leaders network, he has implemented programmes from Leeds Beckett to Lisbon's EKA Palace, from Brussels to Tanzania, all aligned to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals with a target of developing 10,000 young artists, social entrepreneurs, and leaders by 2030. His Face Festival — a youth-led, Global Majority-led international festival — has grown into a ten-day programme encompassing hip-hop dance, Model United Nations, film, and community awards.
At the 2022 Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting in Kigali, Namiuki secured meetings with youth and sports ministers from Ghana to the Maldives, Nigeria to Papua New Guinea — all before the age of thirty. He organised the Rotation Dance Exchange Commonwealth Championships as an official CHOGM sideline event, and led a seven-country mission across Europe to document the experiences of Black and African refugees fleeing the conflict in Ukraine. He approaches the UN Summit of the Future sidelines, the WTO Public Forum, and World Urban Forum with equal ease — testament to a conviction that youth leadership is not a training exercise but a present-tense responsibility.

The Conversation

Panel Themes & Guiding Questions

Theme I

The Economic Outlook: Risk, Opportunity & Structural Transformation

"Is Africa's next growth decade more likely to be driven by structural reform, demographics, or global capital flows?"
  • What are the most misunderstood risks when international investors evaluate African economies? [BIC]
  • What structural transformation does Africa still need to unlock industrial growth? [Dev Econ]
  • How do we transition from the current extractive and unequal growth model to an inclusive one?
  • What is the biggest development misconception policymakers keep repeating?
  • From a policy standpoint, what reforms are most attractive to foreign capital today? [Nigerian HC]
  • What sectors will define Nigeria's next decade?
Theme II

Financial Markets & Capital Mobilisation

"Does debt finance development, or trap countries?"
  • How can governments attract long-term productive FDI rather than speculative flows?
  • Is Africa under-financed, or under-governed? [Open Floor]
Theme III

Politics, Governance & Soft Power

"Can economic transformation occur without political transformation?"
  • Can regional blocs realistically substitute for fragile national institutions? [African Politics]
  • Is political instability overstated in global narratives?
  • Is soft power an underutilised African asset?
Theme IV

Social Norms, Human Capital & Leadership

"What social changes are necessary to unlock Africa's economic potential?"
  • How do we cultivate leadership rather than just aspiration in the African and Afro-descendant youth? [Youth]
  • What narratives must change within our communities?
  • Should development focus first on institutions or on mindset?
Theme V

Africa's Global Influence by 2050

"Will Africa be a participant in the global order, or a shaper of it?"
  • Can Africa act collectively on the international stage?
  • What would need to happen for African markets to be considered 'core' rather than 'emerging'?
  • Can Africa become a net exporter of capital rather than an importer?
  • What role can African art, film, and cultural production play in reshaping the continent's global image?
Lightning Round

Provocations for the Panel

Is growth without institutional reform sustainable?
Does development require democracy?
Are we too dependent on foreign validation — ratings, IMF narratives, Western capital?
Can Africa industrialise in a post-carbon world?
Name one reform Africa must prioritise in the next five years.
